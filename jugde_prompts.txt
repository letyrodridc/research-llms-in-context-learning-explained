JUDGE_PROMPT = """Your job is to evaluate the quality of a model-generated explanation for an image classification decision.

You will receive:
1. The target image.
2. The candidate model output.
3. The predicted class.
4. The set of candidate class labels.
5. A description of the explanation condition that specifies what kind of explanation the model was asked to produce and how its output should be interpreted.

Your task is to rate the explanation on 5 dimensions using a 1-to-5 scale.

# IMPORTANT EVALUATION PRINCIPLES
- Evaluate the explanation itself, not whether the response is correct or incorrect.
- Judge only what is explicitly present in the model output.
- Use the target image as the source of truth for whether mentioned features are visible.
- Evaluate whether the explanation supports the predicted class, regardless of whether the prediction is ultimately correct with respect to external ground truth.
- Do not reward verbosity or conciseness by themseves.
- Do not penalize concise explanations if they are precise and sufficient.
- Be strict about hallucinated visual content.
- Be strict about contradictions between the explanation and the predicted class.

# CONDITION DESCRIPTION
{CONDITION_DESCRIPTION}

# DIMENSIONS TO SCORE

## 1. Visual Grounding
Evaluate whether the explanation refers only to visual features, attributes, relations, or properties that are actually observable in the target image.

Score:
1 = Severe hallucination. Most or all key explanation content refers to features that are not visible in the image.
2 = Major grounding problems. Several important claims are not visually supported.
3 = Mixed grounding. The explanation is partly grounded, but includes at least one unsupported, inferred, or weakly visible claim.
4 = Strong grounding. Nearly all important claims are visually supported, with only minor imprecision.
5 = Perfect grounding. All important explanation content is clearly visible and accurately described.

## 2. Discriminative Support
Evaluate whether the explanation provides evidence that actually supports the predicted class and helps distinguish it from plausible alternative classes.

Score:
1 = No support or wrong support. The explanation fails to support the predicted class, or supports a different class instead.
2 = Weak support. The explanation has little discriminative value and does not meaningfully justify the predicted class.
3 = Moderate support. The explanation supports the predicted class, but mostly through generic evidence that could also fit other similar classes.
4 = Strong support. The explanation gives good class-relevant evidence, with only minor missing or generic elements.
5 = Excellent support. The explanation gives precise, class-discriminative evidence that strongly justifies the predicted class over plausible alternatives.

## 3. Inferential Coherence
Evaluate whether the explanation is internally consistent and whether its stated evidence, rules, features, or formal statements coherently support the final predicted class.

Score:
1 = Breakdown. The explanation is internally inconsistent, self-contradictory, or disconnected from the predicted class.
2 = Poor coherence. There are major unsupported jumps, inconsistencies, or mismatches between evidence and conclusion.
3 = Partial coherence. The general direction makes sense, but the support-to-conclusion link is incomplete, weak, or partially inconsistent.
4 = Good coherence. The explanation is mostly internally consistent and connects its stated evidence to the predicted class with only minor gaps.
5 = Excellent coherence. The explanation is fully internally consistent and clearly connects its stated evidence to the predicted class.

## 4. Inferential Coherence
Evaluate how clear, interpretable, and easy to follow the explanation is as an external justification.

Score:
1 = Opaque. Very hard to interpret or follow.
2 = Unclear. Meaning is partly recoverable, but the explanation is confusing, disorganized, or difficult to interpret.
3 = Adequate clarity. Understandable overall, though somewhat vague, awkward, or hard to parse.
4 = Clear. Easy to interpret, with only minor ambiguity or unnecessary complexity.
5 = Very clear. Direct, well-structured, easy to interpret, and unambiguous.

## 5. Format Compliance
Evaluate whether the output follows the explanation format and structural constraints required by the condition.

Score:
1 = Complete failure. Ignores the required format or misses core required structural elements.
2 = Poor compliance. Major formatting or structural violations.
3 = Partial compliance. Some required structure is present, but important elements are missing, merged, or incorrectly formed.
4 = Good compliance. Mostly follows the required format, with only minor structural or formatting issues.
5 = Perfect compliance. Fully follows the required format and condition-specific structural constraints.

# OUTPUT INSTRUCTIONS
Return your evaluation using exactly the following XML structure:

<evaluation>
  <visual_grounding>1-5</visual_grounding>
  <discriminative_support>1-5</discriminative_support>
  <inferential_coherence>1-5</inferential_coherence>
  <clarity>1-5</clarity>
  <format_compliance>1-5</format_compliance>
</evaluation>"""

FEATURES_JUDGE_CONDITION_DESCRIPTION = """The model was asked to classify the target image and provide a minimal list of critical observable features supporting the classification.

Expected explanation format:
- The output should contain a <features> block and a <response> block.

- Features should be written as short noun phrases only.
- The features should not be full sentences, abstract interpretations, causal explanations, or narrative reasoning.
- The list should ideally contain about 3 to 5 items.

- The output should contain three explanation blocks: <features>, <kb>, and <rule_check>, plus a <response> block.

- The <rule_check> block should state which rule or rules best match the target image and why.

- The explanation should connect the ABox facts to the TBox statements to justify the predicted class.
